## 1. Get Down to Their Level ğŸ‘ï¸

**What:** Position yourself at your child's eye level during interactions.

**Why:** It's easier for your child to see your face, read your expressions, and feel less intimidated.

**How:**
- Sit on the floor during play
- Kneel down when talking
- Bring activities to a low table or coffee table

**Example:** Instead of calling from across the room, go to your child, sit beside them, and engage face-to-face.

## 2. Follow Your Child's Lead ğŸ¯

**What:** Let your child choose the activity and follow their interests.

**Why:** Children communicate more when they're interested and engaged.

**How:**
- Observe what your child is drawn to
- Join them in their play without taking over
- Comment on what they're doing
- Add language to their interests

**Example:** If your child is lining up cars, sit down and join them. Say, "Red car! Blue car! Car goes beep beep!" Don't force them to play "the right way."

## 3. Create Communication Opportunities ğŸ¤”

**What:** Set up situations where your child needs to communicate.

**Why:** Reduces frustration and motivates communication when there's a reason to talk.

**How:**
- Put favorite items in clear containers they can't open
- Place toys on high shelves (in sight but out of reach)
- Give small portions (to encourage asking for more)
- "Forget" essential items (fork at snack time, battery in toy)
- Pause and wait expectantly

**Example:** At snack time, hand your child a sealed bag of crackers. Wait. They may hand it back, reach toward you, make a sound, or even attempt a word. Respond immediately: "Oh! You want help opening it!"

## 4. Use the Power of Pause â¸ï¸

**What:** After you speak or model language, wait silently for 5-10 seconds.

**Why:** Gives your child time to process and respond. Many children need extra time.

**How:**
- Count to 10 in your head
- Look expectantly at your child
- Resist the urge to fill the silence
- Don't repeat immediately

**Example:** 
You: "What do you want? Juice or milk?" 
[PAUSE... count to 10]
If no response, you can offer choices again or help them.

## 8. Use Visual Supports ğŸ“¸

**What:** Pictures, photos, objects, or gestures to support understanding.

**Why:** Many children with autism are visual learners. Pictures make abstract language concrete.

**How:**
- **First-Then boards:** "First snack, then playground"
- **Visual schedules:** Pictures showing the day's activities
- **Choice boards:** Photos of 2-3 options
- **Communication books:** Pictures of favorite things, people, places
- **Gestures:** Point, show, sign

**Example:** Before transitions: Show two pictures: "First cleanup, THEN iPad."

## 9. Celebrate ALL Communication ğŸ‰

**What:** Respond to every communication attempt, whether it's words, sounds, gestures, or eye contact.

**Why:** Builds confidence and encourages more communication attempts.

**How:**
- Acknowledge sounds: "You said something!"
- Respond to gestures: "You're pointing! You want the book!"
- Notice eye contact: "You looked at me! You're ready!"
- Praise attempts: "Good trying to say banana!"
- Give them what they're asking for (whenever possible)

**Example:**
Child reaches toward shelf and makes a sound  
You: "Oh! You want something! What do you see? The ball! Here's the ball!"

## 10. Create Routines and Rituals ğŸ”„

**What:** Predictable activities that happen the same way each time.

**Why:** Routines help children know what to expect and what language to anticipate. Familiarity reduces anxiety.

**How:**
- Use the same words/songs for daily activities
- Bath time routine, mealtime routine, bedtime routine
- Pause at predictable moments for your child to "fill in the blank"
- Make routines fun and engaging

**Example - Bedtime Routine:**
1. Bath time: "Splish splash, time for bath!"
2. Pajamas: "Arms up! Legs in!"
3. Story: Same books with predictable text
4. Song: Same goodnight song every night
5. Pause before the last word: "Goodnight, sweet..." [wait for "baby" or approximation]

## Remember

### Every Child is Unique
What works for one child may not work for another. Try different strategies and see what resonates with your child.

### Progress is Not Linear
Some days will be better than others. Celebrate the small victories and keep going!

### You Don't Have to Be Perfect
Communication opportunities happen naturally when you're having fun and spending time together. Don't stress!

### Consistency is Key
Use these strategies daily. The more you practice, the more natural they'll become.

### Get Support
Work with your SLP to customize these strategies for your child's specific needs and goals.
